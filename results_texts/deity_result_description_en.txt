Gamunjangagi, a sense for where the truth actually begins
In Samgongbonpuri, a youngest daughter named Gamunjangagi is asked by her parents where her fortune comes from. Her older sisters credit the heavens, the earth, and their parents. But Gamunjangagi says her fortune comes from a mark near her belly button called Seongeumot, believed to be the seat of one's innate fortune. Her parents are furious and cast her out. Yet it is Gamunjangagi, who later takes her place as the goddess of fate, governing fortune and destiny across human lives.

Hallakgungi, the sense of returning to what has fallen and setting it right
In Igongbonpuri, Hallakgungi endures harm after harm, yet returns to restore what has been left in ruin and reclaim what was lost. She takes her place as the keeper of Seocheonkkotbat, the Flower Garden of the West, tending to the flowers that hold the fates of human lives.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of what has been broken or left unresolved in the wake of something. There is value placed in tracing a problem back to its origin rather than patching over what's visible on the surface.

Myeongjinguk's Daughter, the sense of creating the conditions for life to arrive safely
In Halmangbonpuri, Myeongjinguk's Daughter becomes the goddess of childbirth, building the safe conditions through which new life can enter the world and grow without harm. She takes her place as the deity who tends to the threshold between the unborn and the living.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong sense of how something new can take root safely in real life. Not simply wanting something to succeed, but actively looking at what conditions, environment, and practical support are needed right now to protect that possibility and help it continue.

Donghae Yonggung's Daughter, the sense of noticing when a beginning hasn't yet found safe ground
In Halmangbonpuri, Donghae Yonggung's Daughter (daughter of the Dragon King of the East Sea) had the power to create life, but not the conditions to protect it once it arrived. What she could bring into being, she could not keep safe. She takes her place as Jeoseung Halmang, the goddess who watches over the boundary between life and its early end.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of whether a new beginning has truly landed on solid ground. When a good idea or a strong force exists, there is a close attention to whether the structure around it is solid enough to hold it.

Chogong Three Brothers, the sense of recognising a new role taking shape around you
In Chogongbonpuri, three brothers leave behind a peaceful life when crisis strikes, taking on the weight and calling given to them to become gods who guide souls. It is not a path they sought out, but one that found them. They take their place as Mujoshin, the ancestral gods of shamans, the ones who first received and carried the sacred calling.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong sense of what a situation is asking of you and how far that responsibility extends. There is a pull toward taking the weight of that seriously, even when it wasn't something you set out to carry.

Noga Danpung Agissi, the sense of seeing the cost behind a beginning
In Chogongbonpuri, Noga Danpung Agissi bore the harshest pressure and the pain of abandonment so that new gods could be born into the world and find their place. What others came to celebrate, she made possible from the shadows. She takes her place as Maengdu, the deity who guards the ritual space and the shamans who work within it.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of who carried what behind a bright beginning or a success. There is a close attention to the invisible weight and cost that others may not think to look for.

Yu Jeongseung's Daughter, the sense of accepting a new role through pressure and change
In Chogongbonpuri, Yu Jeongseung's Daughter faced unexpected relationships and events that shook her life to its foundations, yet she did not turn away from the new fate and role that came to find her. She takes her place as the first shaman, the one who performed the very first ritual.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong sense of where change is moving you and what role it is placing you in. There is a tendency to take seriously what circumstances are asking, rather than holding on to what was familiar.

Gangnim, the sense of tracing what is hidden until the truth comes to light
In Chasabonpuri, Gangnim crosses the boundary between the living and the dead to uncover the true cause of a hidden tragedy and bring back what had been lost. He takes his place as Jeoseung Chasa, the underworld messenger who guides the souls of the dead to the afterlife.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong pull toward what actually lies beneath a conclusion that has already been reached. There is value placed in following something all the way back to its root, rather than accepting what is visible on the surface.

Daebyeolsang Manura, the sense of bringing dangerous forces into order
In Daebyeolsang Manurabonpuri, Daebyeolsang Manura embodies the kind of devastating force that lies beyond human control. Rather than being suppressed or eliminated, she must be received with care and guided into a place where she can no longer cause harm. She takes her place as Mamashin, the deity who governs disease(smallpox), whose power is not destroyed but made safe through the right handling.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong sense of where a powerful or dangerous force sits and how it needs to be handled. There is value placed in finding the right position for something, rather than pushing it out entirely.

Chilseong Agi, the sense of what becomes possible when something unfamiliar is truly welcomed
In Chilseongbonpuri, Chilseong Agi arrives in the form of a snake so unsettling that the world nearly rejects her. But when a woman welcomes her into the household, she takes her place as Chilseongsin, the benevolent snake goddess of wealth, agriculture, and household prosperity.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of how something new and unfamiliar is being received. There is value placed in what an unfamiliar presence might bring once it finds the right place, rather than pushing it away before that place has been found.

Daebyeolwang, the sense of knowing what belongs where
In Cheonjiwangbonpuri, Daebyeolwang cuts through a world in chaos and establishes the principles through which each realm can function on its proper foundation. He takes his place as Jeoseungchaji, the deity who governs the laws and order of the underworld.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of how things are divided, what standards they are divided by, and whether each part is sitting in its rightful place. There is value placed in drawing clear lines and giving things their proper place, rather than carrying the mess forward unresolved.

Sobyeolwang, the sense of recognising when a flawed order has come to pass as normal
In Cheonjiwangbonpuri, Sobyeolwang claims the living world but fails to establish proper law, leaving behind a realm where two suns and two moons rise at once and all manner of chaos and injustice hardens into what passes for the natural order. He takes his place as Iseungchaji, the deity who holds dominion over the world of the living.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of how a problem is operating inside a flawed system that has come to feel like common sense.

Samani, the sense of finding worth in what others have left behind
In Samanibonpuri, Samani tends to the beings that everyone else has abandoned and turned away from. Through that care, he overcomes the threat of death and lives on for three thousand years. He takes his place as Maengam, the deity who wards off death and governs longevity.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of what has been worn down and discarded along the way, and what value still sits unrecognised. When others have moved on and consider something finished, there is value placed in gathering what has been left behind and making it part of a foundation that can continue.

Wongang Ami, the sense of recognising what pain leaves behind and what it demands
In Munjeonbonpuri, Wongang Ami endures hardship and suffering through the roughest turns of her life, yet refuses to let that pain settle as mere tragedy. Instead she carries it as the basis for setting right what was wrong. She takes her place as the mother who tends Seocheonkkotbat (the Flower Garden of the West) where the fates of human lives are kept.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of what a wound has left behind and what it is still asking to be corrected. There is value placed in returning to what remains, even after time has passed, and holding the right people and circumstances to account.

Nokdisaengi, the sense of seeing what is quietly wrong before it surfaces
In Munjeonbonpuri, Nokdisaengi is the first to see through the cunning schemes threatening his household and the hidden dangers running through its relationships. Through clear-headed action, he restores the order of a home that had begun to fall apart. He takes his place as Munjeonsin, the deity who guards the gate of the home.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of what is quietly misaligned beneath a relationship or group that looks fine on the surface. There is value placed in tracing those fractures back to their source and returning things to where they properly belong.

Jijang Agissi, the sense of turning loss into a path through grief
In Jijangbonpuri, Jijang Agissi endures the sudden and devastating pain of loss and separation, yet rather than turning away from that grief, she opens a path for those who are wounded to cross safely into what comes next. She takes her place as Jijangsin, the deity who clears misfortune and guides souls to their proper rest.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of whether a pain has been properly grieved and absorbed, or whether something still lingers unresolved. There is value placed in sitting quietly with what remains rather than accepting a premature ending, giving things the proper closure they deserve.

Jacheongbi, the sense of pushing through until something takes root 
In Seegyeongbonpuri, Jacheongbi passes through meeting, separation, and reunion, and through trial after trial, until she brings the seeds of the five grains down from the heavens to the human world. She takes her place as Seegyeongsin, the goddess who governs agriculture and the sustaining of life.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong sense of how to find a way forward inside a situation that has closed in. There is a pull toward turning that path into something real, into the conditions through which life can actually continue. Rather than stopping at the place that was given, there is value placed in finding what is needed and bringing it back into the world, so that what had stalled can begin to move again.

Yeosan Buin, the sense of sustaining what others don't think to notice
In Jowangbonpuri, Yeosanbuin tends to her family and household through the harshest moments of life. Where others might step back, she keeps feeding, keeps tending, keeps the most basic foundations of living and relationship intact. She takes her place as Jowangsin, the goddess who governs the kitchen and the running of the home.
This result tends to appear when there is a strong awareness of what is quietly holding a situation together beneath the surface. There is a close attention to the everyday care and invisible effort that keeps things from falling apart. When a space or a relationship continues, there is a sense that it does not do so on its own, but only through someone's honest and repeated presence, and that presence is worth seeing.
